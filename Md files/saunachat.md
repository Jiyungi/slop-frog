How an AI detector works (text + image)
For text — three methods:

Perplexity-based: AI text has lower perplexity (more predictable) than human text. Compute log-probability of each token under a reference LLM. Low + uniform = likely AI. Fast but weak.
Stylometric features: AI text has low "burstiness" (sentence length is uniform), high token repetition, fewer typos, smoother transitions. Extract ~20 features, run through a classifier. Better than perplexity alone.
Fine-tuned classifier: Train a small model (e.g. RoBERTa) on a labeled corpus of human vs GPT-4/Claude/Gemini outputs. Best accuracy but needs data + compute.
Most production detectors (GPTZero, Originality.ai, Copyleaks, Winston AI) combine all three.

For images — different problem:

Pixel-level artifact detection: GAN/diffusion models leave subtle frequency-domain fingerprints invisible to humans. CNNs trained on real vs fake pick these up.
Face deepfake detection: Specialized models (Sensity, Reality Defender) look for blink patterns, lip-sync inconsistencies, edge artifacts around faces.
Reverse image search: Check if the image is stolen from a known real person or stock site. Cheap and high-signal.
Metadata: EXIF data, reverse image hash match.
The flow for your Facebook scam tool:

post text ──→ perplexity + stylometric + LLM-judge ──→ "AI?" score
post image ──→ CNN artifact detector + reverse search ──→ "deepfake?" score
account ──→ age, followers, prior reports ──→ "trust?" score
links ──→ URL risk lookup ──→ "malicious?" score
       ──→ combine ──→ final risk verdict (1-10) + top 3 reasons

The honest truth you should know before building one:

AI text detection accuracy is ~50-70% on adversarial text. Paraphrasing defeats it. OpenAI deprecated their own detector in 2023 for this reason.
Image deepfake detection is stronger (~85-90% on known faces, weaker on novel models).
The scam-pattern check (urgency language, financial promises, link risk) is the strongest signal — much more reliable than AI detection.
This is why I keep saying lead with scam detection, not AI detection.
For a 1-day hackathon build: don't train your own detector. Use an existing API (GPTZero, Hive, Reality Defender have free tiers) + your scam-pattern regex + a reverse-image-search call. Glue them together with an LLM judge that reasons over all the signals.

How YouTube's AI label works (rolled out May 2026)
Two signals, used together:

C2PA Content Credentials — cryptographic metadata embedded in the file at creation. Industry-standard provenance signal (Adobe, OpenAI, others support it). If a video file contains valid C2PA metadata saying "this was generated by AI," YouTube auto-applies a permanent, non-removable label. Treated as ground truth.
Proprietary classifiers — YouTube's internal ML models scan for "significant photorealistic AI usage." When detected, label is auto-applied, but creator can appeal if wrong.
Where the label shows up:

Long-form videos: directly below the player, above the description.
Shorts: as an overlay on the video during playback.
What triggers it:

Realistic depictions of real people saying/doing things they didn't.
Realistic scenes of events that never happened.
Content from YouTube's own AI tools (Veo, Dream Screen) — always labeled, permanent.
What does NOT trigger it:

Animated, stylized, unrealistic content.
Routine production help (color grading, captions, SFX).
Why this matters: it's the EU AI Act Article 50 compliance angle — machine-readable provenance is becoming a legal requirement.

Why this doesn't exist everywhere else
Your question "why are there no tools like this" — the real answer is layering:

C2PA works only when AI model creators embed the metadata. Most consumer tools (chatbots, image generators used casually) don't yet.
Classifiers alone (like GPTZero) are too unreliable to make binding labels. YouTube can stack them with C2PA + appeals + human review because they own the platform.
Big platforms (YouTube, Meta, TikTok) have the compute, the corpus, and the leverage to build this. Standalone consumer-facing "AI label" tools that work reliably don't really exist outside those walled gardens — that's the gap your hackathon project could sit in.
